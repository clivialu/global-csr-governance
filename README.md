# Corporate Governance, Law, Culture, Environmental Performance, and CSR Disclosure: A Global Perspective

This repository contains supporting materials for the paper *“Corporate Governance, Law, Culture, Environmental Performance and CSR Disclosure: A Global Perspective”* published in the *Journal of International Financial Markets, Institutions & Money*.

## Paper

- **Title:** Corporate Governance, Law, Culture, Environmental Performance and CSR Disclosure: A Global Perspective  
- **Authors:** J. Lu & J. Wang  
- **Journal:** *Journal of International Financial Markets, Institutions & Money*, 70 (2021)  
- **DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.intfin.2020.101264](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.intfin.2020.101264)  
- **Ranking / Impact:** ABDC A · Impact Factor 6.1
- **Acknowledgement:** SSHRC

## In Plain Language

This research looks at how differences in national governance, legal systems, and cultural environments affect firms’ environmental and CSR behaviors worldwide.  
We find that countries with stronger corporate governance institutions and better legal protections tend to have companies that disclose more CSR information and perform better environmentally.  
Cultural dimensions, such as collectivism and power distance influence how these governance factors translate into sustainability outcomes, suggesting that both institutional and cultural contexts matter for responsible business practices globally.

## Paper Highlight: 

| # | Why it matters | What the paper delivers |
|---|---------------|-------------------------|
| **1** | **Untangles the performance–disclosure knot.** Critics argue green talk can mask greenwash. | Uses a three-stage least-squares (3SLS) simultaneous-equation model to show a positive two-way link between environmental performance (EP) and CSR disclosure, lending support to voluntary-disclosure theory. 
| **2** | **Boards as sustainability engines.** Investors want proof that governance reforms pay off. | Finds that CEO–chair separation, dedicated ESG committees and gender-diverse boards all lift EP and disclosure levels in the following year. 
| **3** | **Debt isn’t just leverage—it’s discipline.** | Shows higher leverage (debt-to-capital) curbs managerial slack and correlates with better EP and richer CSR reporting. |
| **4** | **Global investors reward transparency—legal systems don’t always need to.** | Cross-listed firms outperform on EP and disclosure, while strong legal environments dampen voluntary CSR disclosure, implying regulatory substitutes. |
| **5** | **Culture shapes sustainability outcomes.** | Identifies that low power-distance, feminine, individualistic (for EP), restraint, high uncertainty-avoidance and long-term-oriented cultures consistently advance EP and/or disclosure quality. |
| **6** | **Big-picture evidence.** Practitioners value generalisability. | Analyzes 12,218 firm-years (1,870 firms) across 25 countries (2010-2017), giving rare cross-continent insight. |

### Managerial Takeaways

* **Split the roles, add an ESG committee, and diversify the board.** These three low-hanging governance levers deliver measurable improvements in both real environmental outcomes and the storytelling around them. 
* **Leverage sustainability.** Moderate debt levels act as an internal monitor; tying environmental KPIs to capital-raising discipline can sharpen green performance. 
* **Consider cross-listing to bond with stronger regimes.** International listings appear to boost environmental credibility and reporting depth, especially for firms from weaker governance homes. 
* **Match disclosure strategy to legal context.** In robust legal systems, extra voluntary CSR pages add little value; elsewhere, detailed transparency fills institutional gaps.
